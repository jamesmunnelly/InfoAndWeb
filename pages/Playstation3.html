The PlayStation 3 implemented eliptical curve cryptography to sign it's firmware files. However, they made a fatal mistake in their implementation that cost them tens of millions of dollars and cause irrepareable damage to their company.

The Sony PlayStation 3 was kept secure for longer than any other console sold

Security Downfall

The PlayStation 3 was long thought to be unhackable. After 3 or 4 years, most had given up and others were happy with the linux usage they had. After some hardware dumps of their higher level keys, a team called failoverfl0w were able to discover that Sony used a constant number in place of a random number in their eliptical curve cryptography functions used to sign their firmware.

RFC 1149.5 specifies 4 as the standard IEEE-vetted random number. (source: xkcd.com/221/)

This allowed failoverfl0w to apply some simple mathematics by taking some test data, encrypting it, taking the output and some other known variables used in the cryptography functions, include the mistakenly constant random number and rearrange to solve for Sony's private key.

Public Private Keys! This was the disaster situation for Sony, and it meant that failoverfl0w were able to write their own firmware and sign it in order to make it run on the system.

Sony's recovery from this was a costly three-stage process, which cost countless headaches, millions of dollars and irrepareable damage to Sony's brand name. If Sony had ensured their cryptography functions were following the current standards and were working correctly, with proper random numbers being generated they'd have saved countless damage.
